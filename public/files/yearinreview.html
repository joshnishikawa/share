Tatsuno's Approach to English Instruction in Elementary School

This is an attempt to summarize Tatsuno's approach to teaching English in elementary school. The information is based on my experience over the past year in the position of an ALT and my conversations with other ALTs, teachers, members of the Tatsuno Board of Education and parents. Although I'll be speaking English, the target audience includes all of them and anyone else with an interest in the subject. Any leaking opinions are mine alone.

First of all, I should discuss Tatsuno's approach to teaching English in relation to my understanding of how some other cities handle it.

  • Each city has its own overall approach to teaching English in Elementary school.

    Some cities have English specialists who are responsible for teaching English to 3rd through 6th grades. Some cities have any combination of homeroom teachers, English specialists or English teachers from Jr. high schools come to teach English in elementary schools. This depends on location, number of students and availability of staff.

    Some cities hire enough ALTs to join each class each week. Other cities only hire enough ALTs to join each class once or twice a month.

  • There are different ALT agencies for cities to choose from.

    Each agency has its own hiring standards, employment benefits and training procedures. But, generally, the 'ALT' position is considered a temporary position. This inevitably has some effect on how seriously their role is taken. More on this very complicated aspect a bit later.

  • Different cities have different working relationships with their ALT agencies.

    Although the English curriculum is determined by the prefecture, cities that rely more heavily on ALTs may also rely on their respective agencies for providing lesson plans and teacher training. Some cities also ask ALTs to take on a more active role in the classroom than others. There are even cities that are considering foregoing the ALT position altogether and hiring native English speakers to teach English lessons assisted by the homeroom teachers.

As for Tatsuno...

The city of Tatsuno covers a large area with schools of all sizes. So the teaching situations vary quite a bit. Generally, 3rd and 4th grade homeroom teachers lead English lessons once a week with the ALT assisting each time. 5th and 6th grade classes have English lessons twice a week; The first lesson is taught either by an English specialist or the homeroom teacher alone. The second is taught by the same person assisted by an ALT.

The agency that trains and provides ALTs for Tatsuno also facilitates training sessions for teachers. These sessions are voluntary for homeroom teachers. However, English specialists are required to attend these sessions periodically as well as other training sessions facilitated by the prefecture. This is a balanced and relatively progressive approach.

  • Every student can interact with a native English speaker at least once a week.
  • Homeroom teachers for 3rd and 4th grade make great English speaking role models for their students.
  • Older students get some English instruction exclusively with a Japanese teacher.

    Many students need certain things explained thoroughly as well as opportunities to ask questions without the pressure of being expected to use language they're not comfortable with yet.

  • Then, they have a second lesson to practice what they've learned.

    This has the potential to be very productive and fun. It's also really important to give the students confidence and enthusiasm before they enter the junior high school English program.

It was done a bit differently last year and will be done a bit differently next year as Tatsuno pushes to unify the curriculum between elementary and jr. high school.

Even within the same city, English instruction differs between schools. There are many factors. Among the most significant are...

Each school has a different number of classes in each grade and alltogether. For large schools, planning lessons may be delegated.

I worked at a large school this year with either five or six classes in each grade. For 3rd and 4th grades, it would be impractical to meet with each of 12 homeroom teachers to discuss what to do in class. Instead, one of the homeroom teachers in each grade made the lesson plan for all teachers in that grade. This is how we kept all classes on the same page. However, there are a few problems built in to this approach.

  • Most teachers follow a lesson plan written by someone else.

    The teacher who actually makes the lesson plan with the ALT knows exactly what to do and when. Other teachers receive a copy of the lesson plan but may not understand exactly how to proceed or where they are needed to explain or help demonstrate. Even if it is made clear, there may be several days before the plan is used in the classroom. It's also easy to overlook things that need to be prepared before class.

  • It's difficult to refine the lesson plan because it's already written.

    The ALT gains valuable experience each time they teach the lesson and may find something that needs more clarification or a more fun way to do something. And there isn't often an opportunity to communicate this to the next teacher(s) but doing things off-script may create confusion.

  • The scheduling and rescheduling of lessons can get very chaotic.

    The schedule in Elementary school can be very unpredictable - even more so with more classes. Different classes in the same grade are often at different points in the curriculum. This makes it a bit harder to keep track of what each class needs to be doing.

There are several smaller schools in Tatsuno which face a different set of challenges.

  • The ALT may only be at the school one or two days a week.

    The fewer days an ALT is at a particular school, the fewer opportunities there are to build relationships with students or discuss classes and teaching ideas with other teachers.

  • There may be no opportunity to discuss lesson plans.

    It's usually preferable to plan classes well ahead. However, if the only day to discuss a class is the day of class, the ALT barely has any opportunity to ask questions, discuss ideas for activities or even suggest improvements.

  • The ability to reschedule lessons is very limited.

    It's impossible to reschedule a lesson for a different day the same week because the ALT is only there one day a week. This means there may be large gaps between lessons more often.

In general, classes for learning a foreign language need to be large enough to create a fun atmosphere and limit the amount of pressure on each individual student. They also need to be large enough to allow pair conversation or group work. However, they should be small enough that the overall noise doesn't drown out each person speaking and the teacher is able to give each student attention and feedback.

Most classes in public school are 30~35 students. This is certainly large enough to create a fun low-pressure atmosphere... so much so that the classes can become very difficult to manage.

  • Some challenges that also exist in smaller classes are compounded.

    Some students demand attention and will disrupt the lesson to get it. Others, who also need attention but are less vocal, may feel neglected and lose interest. While these points are also true in smaller classes, in larger classes it becomes far more pronounced when students who cry out for attention have to either combine forces or compete with each other to get the attention they need. Likewise, the larger number of students who are unwilling to ask for help almost guarantees that the teacher will be unable to engage each one of them individually to give them the attention they need.

    In short, it's too difficult to stand out from the crowd and too easy to disappear into it.

  • The approach that makes large classes manageable when teaching other subjects doesn't work well for English lessons.

    A teacher-centered classroom where students face forward and speak only to the teacher only when called upon may be the way to create a manageable classroom setting in situations where the students are expected to solve problems and retain information. However, classes that focus on communication cannot be expected to operate effectively in this way.

    There is great potential for developing a student-centered approach.

  • 3rd and 4th grade classes are considered an 'activity'.

    Classes in these grades are only taught once a week. The homeroom teacher is considered T1 and leads the lesson. The ALT follows their lead as T2. The students are not graded.

  • 5th and 6th grade classes are considered a 'subject'.
    • They are taught twice a week.

      Again, one lesson is led either by the homeroom teacher or an English specialist as T1 (without the ALT). The other lesson has that teacher assisted by the ALT as T2.

    • Students are required to take listening and conversation tests.
    • Students' perfomance in English class is reflected on their report card.

Before I get into this, I need to emphasize how hard all of these teachers are working at their extremely complicated and difficult jobs. I hope my comments are interpreted with the utmost respect for the work they all do.

  • Some homeroom teachers are more proficient in English than others.
  • Some homeroom teachers have enough confidence in their English proficiency.

    Many people (even teachers) hide their English proficiency. They may understand English well enough to lead an English lesson but that doesn't mean they have enough confidence in their own ability to feel comfortable leading an English lesson.

  • Some homeroom teachers would just rather leave it to the pros.

    Proficient in English or not, some would simply rather leave the English instruction to a native speaker or at least someone who specializes in it. This is how other subjects like music and calligraphy are handled. And again, homeroom teachers already handle an enormous load.

  • Many teachers are quite confident and enthusiastic about teaching English.

    Early on, I made the mistake of assuming that everyone would be happy if I took on as much of the responsibility for English lessons as possible. In reality, I was just depriving them of an opportunity to set a good example for their students by taking a more active role in English lessons themselves.

Each ALT has their own idea of what thier role is and should be.

Some ALTs take their role as educators more seriously than the term 'assistant language teacher' recognizes. They want to do anything they can to improve their students' communication skills and be a positive influence on their lives.

Some ALTs are are happy to take on a temporary position sharing their language and culture with children for a year or two while they enjoy the experience of learning a bit about the language and culture of a foreign country themselves.

Of course either (and everything in between) is perfectly fine but, each ALT must deal with the previous experiences and expectations of staff members who have worked with other ALTs.

ALTs may face unreasonable expectations of their ability to lead engaging and productive lessons in such a challenging situation. Assuming that they should be expected to lead the lesson in the first place.

Facing unreasonably low expectations presents a different struggle.

  • It may be difficult to get their ideas and methods put into practice or even try things to find out what works, what doesn't work and why.
  • They may only be asked to flash cards and model pronunciation a few times during class.

    The ALT may spend a lot of the class time just waiting for their cue. It's sometimes difficult to stay engaged in the lesson themselves.

    With the HRT/JTE responsible for setting the pace and flow of the lesson, they will naturally end up modeling some language themselves. Even more so in 5th and 6th grade classes because, for one lesson each week, there is no ALT to ask. It just somes naturally to model the language themselves.

  • Although it may be appreciated and even encouraged, it's not expected that the ALT will develop a meaningful relationship with the students.

    Even if an ALT sticks with the job for more than a year, they are often reassigned to other schools. Homeroom teachers who spend all day every day with their students know their students very well. Even even JTEs who spend two hours a week for a number of years can at least be expected to remember all their students' names. This is not so for ALTs who spend 45 minutes a week with each class of 30+ students for one year (even in classes where the students wear name tags).

It may be difficult to find experienced ALTs or to keep those that gain experience.

Teaching English as a second language to large groups of small children in mixed-level classes is probably the most challenging EFL teaching situation there is. Although the job is certainly available to experienced teachers and it's preferable to sign ALTs who have gained experience, many of those people opt for more lucrative and less strenuous job opportunities.

'Experience' beyond teaching materials and methodology is important too.

The term 'culture shock' refers to the difficulty of getting used to doing and thinking about things differently. Something more akin to culture fatigue starts to set in after a few years of getting used to a cluture that doesn't seem to be getting used to you.

Many ALTs are just serious enough about their work and just experienced enough working in the field that they become frustrated with the lack of influence they have on improving their own situation and the circumstances for their students.

It is difficult to stay optimistic when you always feel like the new guy, or a guest at your workplace. Again, some people are fine with the temporary ALT position but others hope to eventually gain a real sense of job security and a sense that they are an experienced and valued part of a team.

Due to all the variables discussed thus far, ALTs must be very flexible in their approach to teaching.

When an English specialist is leading the class...

Even experienced ALTs have to be comfortable following their lead. The ALT may have many suggestions regarding the curriculum, materials or pedagogy but it's ultimately the JTE who bears responsibility for what the students cover. The JTE teaches another entire lesson solo each week, so they will have a better idea of where those specific students are and how the class needs to be managed.

When the homeroom teacher leads the class...

ALTs need to be comfortable following the lead of the homeroom teacher but I believe they also need to be comfortable leading lessons themselves if necessary.

  • HRTs may be absent unexpectedly.

    Sometimes homeroom teachers get sick and the ALT will already be more prepared than the substitute teacher. English classes could be rescheduled but consistent practice is far better.

  • HRTs may have to deal with emergencies.

    Sometimes homeroom teachers have to deal with emergency situations involving students and you never know when they will happen or how long they will take to resolve.

  • Not all HRTs approach English lessons with the same enthusiasm.

    The concern has been raised that some homeroom teachers are less interested in doing their job where teaching English is concerned. But consider the fact that teaching English isn't a part of the homeroom teachers job in every city. Many have English specialists teaching 3rd and 4th grade. Tatsuno could opt for this approach but it's a step backward in my opinion.

  • Some homeroom teachers simply need more assistance than others.

    If a teacher is willing to lead the class, they certainly should. They may model imperfect pronunciation or make mistakes that a native speaker wouldn't but it's important for students to have a real English-speaking Japanese role model they can look up to.

    However, if a teacher is only going to demonstrate unwillingness to try by saying things like, "I don't speak English" out loud during the lesson, it's better to let someone else lead.

  • The ALT gains experience each time they teach the same lesson.

    Teaching a lesson once gives them an opportunity to make adjustments so that it is smoother, more productive and more fun for subsequent classes. Without this insight, each class has to follow the 'rough draft'. This is a benefit that the English specialists have that could and should be applied to all classes if possible.

  • This is the direction English instruction will eventually need to move anyway.

    It's more efficient for the one person who is going to be present during each lesson to prepare for the lesson rather than each homeroom teacher being more or less prepared and all of them proceeding in different ways because each is more or less willing and more or less able to lead an English lesson to begin with.

    Also, if one person takes responsibility for leading a lesson and another takes responsibility for helping students follow, it's better that the HRT who knows their students and how to deal with them best - who is able to guide and encourage them in their native language be the one helping the students follow along.

All students have a chromebook they can use. The level at which they are put to use in class varies by the school, grade and teacher.

Third and fourth grade classes use the "Let's Try" series of textbooks. These books were introduced in 2011 and are published by Hyogo prefecture. Fifth and sixth grade classes use the "New Horizon" series of textbooks. These books are are more current and have very recently been updated.

The curriculum for teaching English to children at the beginner level will be basically the same regardless of which book is used. But, the activities, media resources and supplementary materials will vary.

The shortcomings of any textbook may include...

  • Target language that is outdated, not useful or simply incorrect.
  • Activities that are confusing, difficult or just not fun.
  • Illustrations and media that may be outdated or irrelevant.
  • Inability to facilitate activities when textbooks go missing.

    Students can look at each others' books but they can't write in them. Teachers have to be able to accommodate students who don't have their textbook or they will be left out of many listening and writing exercises.
    Not having the book means not having the materials (cards) included in the book. Teachers will have to improvise to make sure students can participate in certain conversation activities too.

On one hand Teachers may feel like they're not doing their job if they don't follow the textbook closely enough. On the other hand, they may feel guilty brushing over or skipping things covered in the textbook that seem to confusing or irrelevant.

ALTs have a lot to offer!

While it's important for all students to be exposed to the same basic target language and that language should follow some logical progression, a textbook is just one tool at the teacher's disposal. Teachers must make use of any and all tools at their disposal to facilitate the most engaging and productive lessons possible. ALTs can help...

  • Provide valuable insight into authentic English usage.
  • Introduce more current or more useful vocabulary and phrases.
  • Help make the most of the textbook and all other available resources.
  • Help develop more relevant and engaging activities or methods to make lessons more interesting and productive.
  • Share interesting aspects of their culture and ask students about theirs.
  • Model pronunciation.

I hope I've provided some understanding for how complicated an issue teaching English in elementary school is and how much effort it takes from ALTs, teachers and the Board of Education to make the most of limited classtime and resources and help students get the most out of all that effort.

I haven't even touched on many other challenges...

  • The language barrier between teachers and ALTs.
  • Differences in, not only culture but personality.
  • Students with special needs, students who get themselves stuck in the "I don't speak English" category or students who are very very keen to learn English until in comes to speaking.

There is a lot I've left out but I've tried to be thorough. I've also tried to be more constructive than critical. Although, I've probably missed that mark a few times. I hope anyone who's come across this has gained at least some insight. I look forward to gaining further insight myself as time goes on. Thank you.
